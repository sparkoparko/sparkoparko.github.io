Stories from the Streets of Seattle

Connect is a web application that pairs up houseless adults in Seattle with pen pals. This project is in its second iteration stage.

Duration

Informatics Research Capstone

6 months ~

Role

UX Designer

Context

Throughout this project, “houseless” is used instead of “homeless”. The term is adopted and preferred by people living in a housing unstable world. Home is not restricted to a physical place but rather, can be used to describe community, a comfortable environment, or wherever safety and serenity lie. It is a fluid experience, and anyone can have a home.

My team and I partnered with Operation Nightwatch, a nonprofit organization that provides a free dinner, overnight shelter, and other services to those in need. They asked us to solve an information problem to improve houseless adult’s quality of life.

Secondary Research

Seattle’s Houseless Crisis

My team and I read articles on the crisis to identify information problems and the context around houselessness. We learned that it is caused by structural components, such as the criminal justice system not recognizing systemic racial inequality and policies, and individual.[1]

Longitudinal studies over the past 30 years suggests that the majority of the public was concerned about houselessness. However, attitudes varied by gender, income level, and political affiliation. Participants who identified as Democrat, a woman, and low-income were more likely to report compassion, positive community effects, and belief in structural over individual causes.[2]

01. “Homelessness Response: The Roots of the Crisis”, The City of Seattle, https://www.seattle.gov/homelessness/the-roots-of-the-crisis.

02. 2. Jack Tsai; Crystal Y.S Lee; Jianxuan Shen; Steven M. Southwick; Robert H. Pietrzak, “Public exposure and attitudes about homelessness”, Journal of Community Psychology 47, no. 1 (Jan 2019): 76-92.

Interviews

Understanding our stakeholders

We quickly realized that our project could not solve houselessness or one of its root causes in an instant because they were interconnected. A little stumped, we decided to narrow down after talking to our stakeholders. We had three stakeholders in consideration – homeless adults, Operation Nightwatch, and our mentor Professor Hendry.

All three of our stakeholders valued safety but discussed different aspects and its nuances.

Findings

Houseless people wanted to be and feel secure. One interviewee mentioned, “I wish people can rate shelters. Things like whether they’re well kept, safe, clean, or experienced.”

Operation Nightwatch cared about its volunteers and wanted the houseless to make physically and mentally healthy choices. We noted that there may be value tensions if they discovered houseless visitors doing drugs.

Our mentor and team discussed not being exposed to danger. We acknowledged that because we exist in a community, feeling welcomed, seen, and heard would help create a safe space.

User Research

Interviews

Our mentor told us that marketing associates scope out potential business partners, then suggest them to their managers. I conducted four internal interviews, two with marketing associates and two with managers. Their greatest emphasis was on limited time and a company’s reputation.

“I don’t have a lot of time to check out a website. It’s a pain when the information is all over the place.”

“I make sure the company is reputable somehow before suggesting it to my manager.“

Usability Testing

I also conducted usability tests to better understand the existing website. Four external volunteers who never went on Paradoxes’ website went through three tasks.

01. Find Paradoxes’ services in 3 minutes.

02. Find their clients on a phone.

03. Find their case studies on a desktop.

Final presentation

Pain Points and Recommendations

I discovered that our users, regardless of the device, found the website difficult to navigate and understand. I based my recommendations off of the pain points.

01. Users have limited time, but there's so much information.

Structure the website with an engaging hook, mission statement, and a list of services on the front page. This layout was extremely common among companies of similar sizes.

02. It's difficult to find proof of Paradoxes' credibility.

Include a list of previous clients, case studies, or awards.

03. Vague or technical wording are difficult to understand.

Except for technical marketing terms, explain concepts for a more broader audience.

04. Users aren't sure which tabs lead to what they're looking for.

Conduct a card sort or other tests to reorganize the navigation.

Reflections

If I were to do it again

Once my teammate and I presented our findings to the CEO, COO, and Sr. Marketing Strategist, we handed it over to an external design team. The new web design addressed the pain points outlined above.

In 6 weeks, I learned how to set project milestones and a final deadline. I am more familiar with my pace of work and balancing business goals with user goals. I learned to value the data collection stage, as I found myself referring to it instead of drawing assumptions from my beliefs.

If I were to repeat this project, I would advocate for the human-centered design process. Although the web design addressed the pain points, it changed two weeks into the research phase. I also would recruit marketing associates and their managers external to the company to gather randomized data. While the findings are a reflection of real users, it may be biased because they work for the company.
