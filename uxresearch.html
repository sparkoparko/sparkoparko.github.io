Crafting an Engaging Website

Paradoxes is a market research company expanding clientele in the tech industry. I made recommendations based on UX research on how to help capture a potential client's attention.

Context

Summer 2019 internship

6 weeks

Role

UX Researcher

Team of 2 with a mentor

Links

Website

Founded in 2007, Paradoxes prides itself on delivering quick, credible recommendations. The company developed strong relationships with its clients over the years and it was ready to expand its network beyond word of mouth.

A fellow intern and I were asked to draft recommendations on how Paradoxes could distinguish itself from its competitors.

Competitive analysis

Understanding the Market

size
location
services

We were given a list of 24 competitors. The companies were sorted by proximity in size, location, and services and analyzed by each group and as a whole. Competitive analysis helped identity their strengths and weakness and guided our understanding of how Paradoxes fared in comparison to others.

Findings

01. Branding and marketing as thought leaders in tech

02. Responsive web design

03. Proof of credibility – case studies, awards, etc.

04. Web information architecture optimized for a quick glance

Based on this information, we decided to focus on how Paradoxes' website could be redesigned.

User Research

Interviews

Our mentor told us that marketing associates scope out potential business partners, then suggest them to their managers. I conducted four internal interviews, two with marketing associates and two with managers. Their greatest emphasis was on limited time and a company’s reputation.

“I don’t have a lot of time to check out a website. It’s a pain when the information is all over the place.”

“I make sure the company is reputable or I have connections before suggesting it to my manager.“

Usability Testing

I also conducted usability tests to better understand the existing website. Four external volunteers who never went on Paradoxes’ website went through three tasks.

01. Find Paradoxes’ services in 3 minutes.

02. Find their clients on your phone.

03. Find their case studies on a desktop.

Final presentation

Pain Points and Recommendations

I discovered that our users, regardless of the device, found the website difficult to navigate and understand. I based my recommendations off of the pain points.

01. Users have limited time, but there's so much information.

Structure the website with an engaging hook, mission statement, and a list of services on the front page. This layout was extremely common among companies of similar sizes.

02. It's difficult to find proof of Paradoxes' credibility.

Include a list of previous clients, case studies, or awards.

03. Vague or technical wording are difficult to understand.

Except for technical marketing terms, explain concepts for a more broader audience.

04. Users aren't sure which tabs lead to what they're looking for.

Conduct a card sort or other tests to reorganize the navigation.

Reflections

If I were to do it again

Once my teammate and I presented our findings to the CEO, COO, and Sr. Marketing Strategist, we handed it over to an external design team. The new web design addressed the pain points outlined above.

In 6 weeks, I learned how to set project milestones and a final deadline. I am more familiar with my pace of work and balancing business goals with user goals. I learned to value the data collection stage, as I found myself referring to it instead of drawing assumptions from my beliefs.

If I were to repeat this project, I would advocate for the human-centered design process. Although the web design addressed the pain points, it changed two weeks into the research phase. I also would recruit marketing associates and their managers external to the company to gather randomized data. While the findings are a reflection of real users, it may be biased because they work for the company.
