Storytelling through social media

Confronting Stigma of Failure Within the Asian American Community

Overview

The pressure to achieve the American Dream within the Asian American community leaves little room for disappointing loved ones and oneself. To address the stigma of failure, I shared on Instagram Stories how a detour in my life trajectory led to growth and resilience.

Context

6-weeks long

Personal project

Role

Storyteller

UX Designer

The challenge

encourage people on social media to view an issue differently

I chose Instagram, a visual application that allows its users to share moments through pictures and videos. My private account allows me to selectively choose who consumes my content, meaning that I can interact with a close group of friends. Instagram is also the 2nd most used social platform after Facebook, yet generates 4x more interactions through its Instagram Stories. Next, I researched issues my Instagram followers would view as relevant and problematic.

Understanding my Instagram users

The Research

+ Data Analysis

I analyzed my followers and discovered a few interesting insights.

  • 90% are Asian American
  • 67% identify as women
  • 67% are friends from college. Out of this, 51% are from one student organization.

Based on these findings, I decided to create relevant content for Asian Americans in their early to mid-'20s.

+ Persona Spectrum

As an avid reader on Medium, I read an article on how personas are an average of attributes that we imagine an average customer to have. Persona spectrums are different - they acknowledge diversity, capabilities, and changing contexts. I took the physical and social context into account. A follower may be at home, in a car, library, on the bus, or out in the city. They may be surrounded by a crowd, alone, with coworkers, or family. Given these scenarios, I decided the content should:

  • highlight key points
  • be family-friendly
  • minimize rotating the screen from one story to the next

+ Interviews

Similar to how every community is different, the Asian American population is no exception. It is composed of 20 countries in East and Southeast Asian and the Indian subcontinent. Among these ethnic groups, the wealth and education gap persist today.

  • What problematic issues exist within your Asian American community?
  • How does it impact you or the people around you?

I asked 20 Asian American friends about their experience to gain insight without generalizing the Asian American population.

The most common topic was the pressure to achieve the American Dream. Due to the label as a model minority, Asian Americans are expected to academically outperform and work more than students from all other minority groups in classes, co-curricular activities, and part-time jobs. This societal pressure is internalized and reinforced at home when immigrant family members hold high expectations. Unfortunately, the model minority myth discourages students from seeking support for services on campus and continues a cycle of distress. Research shows that Asian immigrant families "have closed communication patterns and rigid hierarchical relationships", leading to conflict and a lack of support. One interviewee recounted the suicide clusters in affluent high schools in the Bay Area in California.

Brainstorming, Iterating...

The Design Process

The pressure to succeed is a grim, emotional topic. However, I approached it with a more positive outlook by suggesting ways it led to personal growth, resilience, and a stronger community.

+ Iterations

I started with a general outline of my story and created a warm palette that evoked a sense of comfort. Before arriving at the final design, I went through 2 design iterations and received feedback at each stage.

The final product

An Instagram Story on Resilience

Below, I reflected on the highs and lows of my junior year.

Storytelling: I highlighted personal growth and resilience by explaining distressing events and how I overcame them. I emphasized close friends that supported me throughout the year, instead of many.

Experience: I used minimal words per line so viewers could skim through and still understand the message.

Aesthetic: I designed with warm tones and visualized comfort and nostalgia.

What I've learned and moving forward

Reflections

In one month, I grew as a designer. I edited pictures on Adobe Lightroom for a consistent aesthetic. Then, I used Adobe Photoshop to create mockups for the iteration phase. Later I used the same skill to create cover pictures throughout my portfolio. I'm learning how to take in mixed feedback and make design-related decisions, as I got mixed reviews.

Throughout this project, I worried about people's perceptions of my vulnerability and message. Did my story encourage others to reflect on the internal and external pressures in their life? Or were there too many photos? While it was difficult to gauge my followers' impressions, I was proud to reach 76% of them.

I enjoyed the design aspect of this project and how it allowed me to incorporate my Sociology degree. If I were to repeat this project, I would focus on mental health awareness among Asian Americans. My research showed that model minority myth and an unsupportive community creates an unsafe environment for students to seek treatment.
